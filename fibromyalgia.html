Case 2: The Anxious Designer with Overlapping Syndromes

Visit 1

A 34-year-old female graphic designer with a known history of irritable bowel syndrome (IBS-alternating type) and generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) presents for evaluation of worsening pain and "brain fog." For the last 6-8 months, she has noticed a gradual onset of diffuse muscle aches, particularly in her upper back, neck, and hips. "It's not just my stomach anymore; now everything hurts," she says. She also reports significant cognitive difficulties, stating, "I'm missing deadlines because I can't focus, and I keep forgetting client feedback. It's really scaring me." Her sleep is poor, and she feels tired "down to my bones." Her anxiety has worsened, as she is convinced she has "something terrible, like lupus or MS."

Past Medical History: IBS since her early 20s, GAD diagnosed 5 years ago.

Medications: Dicyclomine PRN for abdominal cramps, Lorazepam 0.5 mg PRN for panic attacks (uses 1-2 times per week).

Vitals & Physical Exam: BP 130/85, HR 88, T 98.7¬∞F, BMI 22. Appears anxious and fatigued. Musculoskeletal: Normal joint exam. Widespread tenderness to palpation with light pressure over her back, arms, and legs. Abdomen: Soft, non-distended, with mild diffuse tenderness but no guarding or rebound.

Question 1: Given her history of IBS and GAD, which historical domains are most crucial to explore to understand the potential underlying pathophysiology of her current symptoms? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: All are important for understanding a potential central sensitivity syndrome (CSS). (a), (b), and (c) explore risk factors and comorbid conditions common in the CSS family. (d) helps establish the pattern of overlapping and mutually exacerbating conditions. (e) While specific to IBS, managing IBS symptoms is a key part of managing the overall symptom burden in a patient with multiple CSSs.

Visit 2 (2 weeks later)

Initial labs (CBC, CMP, TSH, CRP, ESR, ANA) are all negative. You explain to the patient that her symptoms, in the context of her known IBS and GAD, are highly suggestive of fibromyalgia, another condition in the family of central sensitivity syndromes. You introduce the concept of central sensitization as a shared mechanism. The patient is relieved that it is not lupus but is skeptical. "So you're saying it's all connected? How can my gut and my mood affect pain in my muscles?"

Question 2: Which of the following are key components of a multidisciplinary management plan for this patient, addressing her overlapping conditions? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: A multidisciplinary approach is essential. (a) CBT is effective for fibromyalgia, anxiety, and IBS. (b) A low FODMAP diet is an evidence-based treatment for IBS, and managing IBS can reduce the overall symptom burden. (c) Mind-body exercises are recommended for fibromyalgia and can also help with anxiety. (e) Self-management strategies for sleep and stress are foundational. (d) Strong opioids are contraindicated in fibromyalgia and would not address the underlying central sensitization.

Visit 3 (4 months later)

The patient has been working with a therapist and has found yoga to be helpful for her stress levels. Her IBS is better controlled with dietary changes. However, her widespread pain, fatigue, and "fibro fog" remain significant barriers to her work and quality of life. She is open to trying a medication that might help with both her pain and her underlying anxiety. You decide to initiate duloxetine.

Question 3: You are starting duloxetine 30 mg daily. Which of the following represents the most accurate and comprehensive counseling for this medication? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: This is a comprehensive counseling session. (a) explains the dual mechanism of action relevant to her comorbid conditions. (b) sets expectations for common, manageable side effects. (c) addresses a critical safety warning. (d) provides important information about stopping the medication. (e) validates the choice of an FDA-approved agent and reinforces why it was selected for her specific symptom profile.

Case 2 Conclusion

This case highlights the common overlap between fibromyalgia and other central sensitivity syndromes (IBS) and mood disorders (GAD). It emphasizes the importance of a multidisciplinary approach, including CBT and dietary management, and the strategic selection of pharmacotherapy (SNRIs) that can address multiple symptom domains simultaneously.

Case 3: The Retired Worker with Worsening Pain

Visit 1

A 62-year-old retired construction worker with a 10-year history of bilateral knee osteoarthritis (OA) presents with a 6-month history of worsening, diffuse pain. "My knees have always hurt, doc, but this is different. Now my shoulders, my back, my arms... everything aches. The stiffness in the morning is brutal; it takes me a good two hours to get moving." He also reports overwhelming fatigue and sleeping poorly. His wife prompted the visit, noting he has become more withdrawn and has stopped going on his morning walks. He denies any history of depression.

Past Medical History: Osteoarthritis, hypertension, hyperlipidemia.

Medications: Lisinopril 20 mg daily, Atorvastatin 40 mg daily, Acetaminophen 1000 mg up to TID for knee pain.

Vitals & Physical Exam: BP 132/80, HR 68, T 98.4¬∞F, BMI 29. Musculoskeletal: Crepitus and mildly restricted range of motion in both knees, consistent with OA. No warmth, erythema, or effusion. No synovitis in the MCPs, wrists, or shoulders. He reports significant pain with palpation of the shoulder and hip girdles.

Question 1: Given this patient's age and the nature of his symptoms (prominent shoulder/hip girdle pain and stiffness), which of the following physical exam maneuvers are most critical to perform to narrow the differential diagnosis? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: The primary goal is to differentiate an inflammatory process like PMR or late-onset RA from a non-inflammatory one like fibromyalgia. (a) The presence of synovitis would strongly suggest an inflammatory arthritis over fibromyalgia. (d) Assessing for proximal muscle weakness is important to screen for inflammatory myopathies. (e) Palpating the temporal arteries is a crucial screening for giant cell arteritis, which is associated with PMR. (b) In PMR, active range of motion is typically limited by pain and stiffness, whereas in fibromyalgia it is often preserved despite pain. (c) The formal tender point exam is no longer required for diagnosis and has been replaced by symptom-based criteria.

Question 2: What is the most appropriate initial laboratory workup for this patient? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: The labs are aimed at ruling out key mimics. (a) ESR and CRP are essential; they would be expected to be elevated in PMR but normal in fibromyalgia. (b) Hypothyroidism and Vitamin D deficiency can mimic fibromyalgia symptoms. (c) RF and anti-CCP are needed to screen for late-onset RA. (d) CK is important to rule out an inflammatory myopathy. (e) The WPI/SSS are diagnostic tools, not laboratory tests, and would be administered after the initial exclusionary workup is complete.

Visit 2 (1 week later)

The patient's lab results return. His ESR is 12 mm/hr (normal <20) and CRP is 0.4 mg/dL (normal <0.8). TSH, Vitamin D, RF, anti-CCP, and CK are all normal. These results make PMR and RA highly unlikely. You explain that it is very common for people with a chronic pain condition like osteoarthritis to develop a second, overlapping condition called fibromyalgia, which amplifies pain throughout the body.

Question 3: The patient is resistant to the idea of another diagnosis. "So my arthritis is spreading? I thought it was just in my knees." Which educational approach is most appropriate? (Select the best answer)

Rationale: (a) is the best patient-centered explanation. It validates his experience, differentiates the new diagnosis from his existing one, introduces the concept of central sensitization in simple terms, and explains how the two conditions can interact. (b) is invalidating and inaccurate. (c) is incorrect; corticosteroids are not effective for fibromyalgia and his labs do not support an inflammatory diagnosis. (d) While technically correct, simply stating the diagnostic rule is not an effective educational or alliance-building strategy.

Visit 3 (3 months later)

The patient was referred to physical therapy and found that aquatic therapy was very helpful for his knee pain, but the diffuse muscle pain, stiffness, and fatigue have not improved. He is having significant trouble with sleep. He is willing to consider a medication but is wary of side effects. "I'm on enough pills as it is," he says. You discuss starting pregabalin, explaining that it is not an antidepressant and is FDA-approved for fibromyalgia.

Question 4: When counseling the patient on starting pregabalin 75 mg twice daily, what are the most important points to cover? (Select all that apply)

Rationale: (a) Explaining the mechanism simply helps the patient understand why a non-opioid, non-antidepressant is being used. (b) Setting clear, realistic goals for the medication is key. (c) and (d) are crucial for safety and managing expectations about common and important side effects. (e) is incorrect; pregabalin requires titration and its effects are gradual, not immediate. Setting an expectation of complete relief is also unrealistic.

Case 3 Conclusion

This case addresses the challenge of diagnosing fibromyalgia in the presence of a comorbid pain condition (OA) and the need to differentiate it from inflammatory mimics like PMR in older adults. It illustrates the use of gabapentinoids (pregabalin) as an alternative to antidepressants for managing pain and sleep.

Guideline Summary and References

üéØ Core Principles of Management

International consensus guidelines uniformly advocate for a patient-centered, individualized, and multidisciplinary approach. The goal is to improve function and quality of life, recognizing that complete pain elimination is often unrealistic.

  • Non-Pharma First: Management should prioritize active patient participation and self-management strategies.
  • Biopsychosocial Approach: Address physical health, psychological well-being, and social functioning.
  • Graduated Treatment: Start with foundational therapies (education, exercise) and add pharmacotherapy or specialized therapies as needed for severe symptoms.

Top 3 International Guidelines for Fibromyalgia Management

1. EULAR (European) Guidelines (2017)

EULAR revised recommendations for the management of fibromyalgia.

Key Feature: Provides a highly influential, evidence-based framework for a "stepped-care" approach. It is the only guideline to issue a "Strong For" recommendation for exercise as the cornerstone of therapy.

2. Canadian Pain Society Guidelines (2012/2013)

Canadian Pain Society and Canadian Rheumatology Association recommendations for the management of fibromyalgia.

Key Feature: Emphasizes individualized treatment based on the most bothersome symptoms (pain, fatigue, sleep, mood). Provides strong recommendations for CBT and multicomponent therapy alongside pharmacological options.

3. AWMF (German) Guidelines (Updated 2017)

Definition, pathophysiology, diagnosis and treatment of fibromyalgia syndrome.

Key Feature: Provides detailed recommendations on a wide range of pharmacological and non-pharmacological therapies. Offers strong recommendations for aerobic exercise, CBT, and meditative movement therapies (e.g., Tai Chi, Yoga).

Note on ACR (American College of Rheumatology)

While crucial, the ACR guidelines primarily focus on diagnostic criteria (2016 revisions) rather than comprehensive management recommendations. They define how fibromyalgia is diagnosed (using WPI and SSS, moving away from tender points) and clarify that FM can coexist with other conditions.

üåü Take-Home Message

The management of fibromyalgia requires a comprehensive, multidisciplinary approach. All major guidelines agree that exercise is the most effective intervention and should be the foundation of treatment. Pharmacotherapy plays an adjunctive role for severe symptoms, but non-pharmacological strategies are essential for long-term functional improvement.
